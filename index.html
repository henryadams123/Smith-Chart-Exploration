WHAT IT IS

A protractor for reflection.

Every impedance you could plug into a 50 Ω line reflects some fraction of the incident signal back toward the source. That fraction is the reflection coefficient, Γ, a complex number with magnitude between 0 and 1. The Smith chart is simply the Γ-plane, with a unit circle boundary, redrawn so that instead of reading off Γ directly you read off the impedance that produced it.

Phillip Smith built it in 1939 as a way to do impedance-to-reflection-coefficient conversions without a slide rule full of complex arithmetic. Two families of circles are overlaid on the Γ-plane: constant-resistance circles, all tangent to each other at the open-circuit point, and constant-reactance arcs, all radiating from that same point. Find where an r-circle crosses an x-arc and you've found z = r + jx, plotted at the exact spot on the unit disk where its reflection coefficient lives. No calculator required, and every transformation a transmission line or a lumped element performs on that impedance turns into a clean geometric move: a rotation, or a slide along one of those curves.

HOW TO READ IT

Three things to find first.

R = CONST

Resistance circles

Each circle holds one value of normalized resistance r, from r = 0 (the outer boundary, a short circuit) to r = ∞ (a single point, the open circuit, at the far right). r = 1 is the circle that passes through dead center, which is why it matters so much for matching.

X = CONST

Reactance arcs

Arcs bending toward the top of the chart carry positive, inductive reactance. Arcs bending toward the bottom carry negative, capacitive reactance. The horizontal line through the center is x = 0, purely resistive.

|Γ| = 1

The outer boundary

Every point on the rim reflects 100% of the incident signal: a short, an open, or a pure reactance. The closer a point sits to the center, the better matched it is, and the less it reflects.

WHERE IT'S USED

Four everyday jobs.

01

Matching networks

Design the inductors, capacitors, or stubs that walk an awkward load impedance back to the center of the chart, where it stops reflecting.

02

VSWR & return loss

Read |Γ| straight off a point's distance from center, then convert to VSWR or return loss without touching a formula.

03

Stub tuning

Moving along a length of transmission line rotates a point around the chart's center, which is exactly how open- and short-stub tuners are designed by hand.

04

Amplifier stability

Stability and gain circles are drawn directly on Smith chart axes to show which source and load impedances keep an amplifier from oscillating.
